The Life of B.B. Warfield

A Scholarly Giant with a Shepherd's Heart

Summary Biography Theology Private Life The Archive

Benjamin Breckinridge Warfield (1851–1921) is widely considered the last of the great "Princeton Theologians." For thirty-four years, he stood as a bulwark of Reformed orthodoxy against the rising tides of modernism and theological liberalism in the United States.

Early Influences

Born into a wealthy Kentucky family, Warfield was the grandson of the famous preacher Robert Jefferson Breckinridge. His early education was surprisingly scientific; he was an avid collector of birds and an expert in shorthorn cattle breeding. This scientific rigor never left him—he approached theology not as a mystic, but as a systematician who believed that truth was objective and discoverable.

1871 Graduation from Princeton - Graduated with highest honors in mathematics and science.
1876 Ordination - After studying at Princeton Seminary and in Leipzig, Germany, he entered the Presbyterian ministry.
1887 The Chair of Theology - Succeeded A.A. Hodge as the Professor of Didactic and Polemic Theology at Princeton.

Theological Contributions

Warfield is most famous for his defense of the Inerrancy of Scripture. He argued that the Bible was "concursively" produced—meaning God used the distinct personalities and styles of human authors to produce a text that was, in its original autographs, entirely free from error.

"The Bible is the Word of God in such a sense that whatever it says, God says." — B.B. Warfield

Beyond inerrancy, Warfield was a champion of "Old School" Presbyterianism. He wrote extensively on the work of the Holy Spirit, the divinity of Christ, and the Plan of Salvation. Interestingly, he was also open to a form of "Theistic Evolution," believing that God could have used natural processes to bring about the human body, provided that the soul was a direct divine creation.

The Secret Burden

While Warfield was known as a fierce "lion" in print, his private life was defined by extreme tenderness. In 1876, while on their honeymoon in Germany, he and his wife Annie were caught in a violent thunderstorm. Annie suffered a trauma (likely a stroke or nervous system collapse) that left her an invalid for the rest of her life.

For the next 39 years, Warfield rarely left her side. He did not travel to conferences or take vacations; he did his world-class research in a study just rooms away from his wife so he could care for her every need. This devotion adds a layer of deep empathy to his otherwise rigorous intellectual work.
